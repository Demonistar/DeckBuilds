ELEVATOR PITCH  (30 seconds)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The Finance module turns your AI companion into a personal financial analyst
that actually pays attention.

Drop in a receipt. The system already knew your budget, the day of the week,
the seasonal trend on your electric bill, and your retirement trajectory.
Now it knows where the $19.76 at Taco Bell fits in. If your fast food habit
is starting to dig into next month's savings, you don't get a generic warning.
The AI walks up and says: "Hey, groceries were fine, but fast food's eating
the budget. Want to adjust, or accept the savings hit?"

Three workspaces. Budget, Vision, Retirement. Each one drillable down to
which Tuesdays you bought fast food at McDonald's. Every transaction

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FULL MARKETING PITCH
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

> Built for the person who has never used a budget tool and doesn't know
> what a Roth IRA is. Built deep enough that the person who runs financial
> models for a living will still find something to like.


The problem with personal finance software is that it lies to you by
omission. It shows you what you spent. It doesn't show you why, when, with
whom, or what it cost you in the long run. It tells you you're 60% through
your food budget. It doesn't notice that 80% of that came from one merchant
on Fridays, and it never asks if you want to change that.

The Finance module for Echo Deck is built around a different promise:
your financial life is data, and data is only valuable if someone (or
something) is actually looking at it.


-------- THE THREE WORKSPACES --------

Budget lives in workspace one. Sub-tabs for Weekly, Monthly, Annual, and
the raw Ledger. The Weekly view shows you where this week's money is going
in real time. The Monthly view applies the budget method you choose (50/30/20,
50/20/30, zero-based, pay-yourself-first, envelope, or none) and overlays
the right visualization. The Annual view projects the rest of the year
using your actual data, with year-over-year overlay once you've been
running the system long enough to have that history. The Ledger view is
your full transaction database, Excel-style, every row editable with a
deliberate click-to-edit-and-save workflow.

Financial Vision lives in workspace two. Current vs Goal across every
category. Savings Goals with progress bars, pacing, ETA, and on-track
indicators. Emergency Fund tracker built around the 3-6-9 rule, with the
target dollar amount auto-derived from your actual monthly expenses. Debt
panel with avalanche or snowball ordering and per-debt payoff estimates.

Retirement Planning lives in workspace three. Eighteen input fields, each
with a help icon, walking through current state, retirement state, and
uncertainty parameters. A 45-year projection. A balance chart with under
and over uncertainty bands. Sensitivity tiles: "what if my return were 5%?
what if I saved $5,000 more annually? what if I worked until 67?" Each one
re-renders the chart on click without committing the change.


-------- THE LEDGER PHILOSOPHY --------

Nothing is ever deleted. Nothing is ever overwritten. Every monetary event,
every setting change, every budget threshold adjustment writes a new row
with a timestamp. Edits happen as supersede rows behind the scenes. Voids
flip a flag, they don't remove the data.

Why does this matter? Because year-over-year analysis is only honest when
the data behind it is honest. Because when you change your food budget
from $200 to $250 in July, the June view should still show $200 (because
that's what it was in June). Because real financial decisions need real
history.

The user never sees this. They see Edit, Save, Delete buttons that just
work. The forensic integrity is invisible until it matters.


-------- THE AI COACH --------

Every methodology choice, every input field, every projection assumption
has a help icon. Click it: get a plain-English explanation. Click "Ask AI
to explain": get a coaching session with the active persona's voice, full
context of your finance state, and clickable follow-up questions to keep
the conversation going.

The AI remembers. Six months from now, when you click the same help icon,
the AI says: "Last June we talked about this and you decided to keep it
at 6.25%. Your portfolio has grown to $X since then. Want to revisit?"
Conversation continuity is built in via session tracking, topic keys, and
context snapshots. Sessions close on satisfaction, idle timeout, module
switch, or detected topic shift.

When a budget threshold trips, the AI doesn't pop a modal that says "you
spent too much." It looks at the actual transactions, the historical
averages, the savings impact, and produces something useful: "Groceries
were within plan this week, but fast food jumped 290% above your average.
You have $36 left in food for the next three days. Want to raise the
budget (which means $50 less to savings), cut fast food this weekend,
or ride it out?"


-------- DRILL-DOWN ON EVERY CHART --------

Double-click a slice of the Food category donut. Now you're looking at
Food broken down: Groceries, Fast Food, Dining Out. Double-click Fast
Food. Now you see Taco Bell, McDonald's, Burger King, ranked by spend.
Double-click McDonald's. Now you see a line chart of every McDonald's
purchase you've made this year, point by point, day by day.

Right-click any chart point: "Send this to AI." The AI doesn't get raw
numbers; it gets the drill path, the filter context, your broader budget
state, and any prior conversations on related topics. It says something
useful.

Drill state persists per workspace. Switch to Retirement, come back to
Budget, you're still looking at McDonald's-on-Fridays. The view-mode
toggle is drill-aware: switching from KPI to Data shows you the ledger
pre-filtered to exactly the rows you were just charting.


-------- WHAT IT DOES THAT OTHER APPS DON'T --------

Most personal finance apps stop at "track and categorize."

This module:

  - Treats your data as append-only history, not mutable state, so
    year-over-year actually compares apples to apples.

  - Supports five real budgeting methodologies as first-class visualizations,
    not just labels. The Monthly view literally renders differently
    depending on which method you've picked.

  - Lets you drill from a budget donut down to "which Fridays at McDonald's"
    in three clicks.

  - Auto-matches actual transactions to planned recurring entries so
    variance becomes a queryable insight, not a guess. Surfaces trend-based
    plan suggestions: "Your electric bill has averaged $147 the last six
    months. Want to update your plan from $120 to $147?"

  - Surfaces day-of-week and time-of-day spending patterns so you can see
    the actual shape of your habits.

  - Couples threshold breaches to an AI that has context: your budget,
    your savings rate, your prior coaching conversations on similar issues,
    the active persona's voice.

  - Carries help content for every input field, with two-tier explanation
    (short plus detailed plus worked example) and Ask AI as the escape hatch.

  - Keeps automatic monthly and quarterly snapshots plus manual per-workspace
    snapshots, so you can compare January-you to August-you.

  - Tracks pending checks separately from cleared so budget tracking happens
    immediately but display balance reflects actual cleared money.

  - Computes the retirement projection iteratively with three scenarios
    (base, under, over) and renders confidence bands you can toggle on
    and off.

  - Sensitivity tiles let you test what-if changes (return rate, savings
    amount, retirement age) without committing them.

  - Lives entirely on your machine. No bank API. No cloud sync. No data
    leaving your system. Manual entry is a feature, not a limitation.


-------- KEY FEATURES (BULLETED) --------

  Architecture
  - Single self-contained Finance.py packaged via edm_packager.py
  - Claims three workspace slots (Budget / Vision / Retirement)
  - One database: finance.db in DeckRoot/Finances/
  - Append-only ledger, append-only settings history
  - Computed account balances (never stored)
  - Daily automatic backup with 30-day retention
  - Pre-migration backups that never rotate
  - Clean release() lifecycle (timers stopped, signals disconnected,
    widgets destroyed properly, DB closed)

  Budget Workspace
  - Weekly sub-tab: pacing, transactions, reconciliation
  - Monthly sub-tab: method-specific visualization
  - Annual sub-tab: predictive + year-over-year + spending patterns

  - Five budget methods: 50/30/20, 50/20/30, zero-based,

  - Per-category thresholds with three warning modes (silent, banner,
    AI nudge)
  - Need / Want / Savings-Debt tiers on every transaction
  - Planned vs Actual variance with auto-match suggestions
  - Trend-based plan suggestions when variance exceeds 20% over 6 months
  - Pending and cleared check states
  - Weekly reconciliation workflow

  Vision Workspace
  - Current vs Goal across seven Vision categories

  - Emergency Fund panel with 3-6-9 rule and auto-derived target

  - Balance projection chart with toggleable uncertainty bands
  - Salary and savings trajectory chart
